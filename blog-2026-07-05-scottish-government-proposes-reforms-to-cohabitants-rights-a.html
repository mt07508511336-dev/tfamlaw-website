Scottish Government Proposes Reforms to Cohabitants' Rights After Partner's Death

5 July 2026

The Scottish Government has recently initiated a consultation aimed at reforming the financial rights of cohabitants when a partner dies without a will. This move seeks to address concerns that the current legal framework may not adequately support individuals who have lived together without formalizing their relationship through marriage or civil partnership.

Under the existing provisions of the Family Law (Scotland) Act 2006, cohabitants can apply to the court for financial provision from their deceased partner's estate. However, they must do so within six months of the partner's death. Many have argued that this timeframe is too restrictive, especially during a period of grief and adjustment.

The proposed reforms include extending the application period from six to twelve months, aligning it more closely with other legal timeframes and providing cohabitants with a more reasonable window to make their claims. This change aims to offer better support and recognition to cohabiting partners, reflecting the evolving nature of family structures in Scotland.

It's important to note that these reforms are still in the consultation phase, and the government is seeking input from the public and stakeholders to shape the final legislation. As family law continues to evolve, staying informed about these developments is crucial for those who may be affected.
