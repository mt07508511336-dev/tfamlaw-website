Scottish Government Proposes Reforms to Cohabitation Rights

6 July 2026

In recent years, the dynamics of family life in Scotland have evolved significantly, with more couples choosing to live together without formalizing their relationship through marriage or civil partnership. Recognizing this shift, the Scottish Government has initiated a consultation aimed at reforming the legal framework to better protect cohabiting couples, particularly concerning financial matters upon separation and rights in cases of intestacy.

Currently, cohabiting couples in Scotland have limited legal rights compared to their married counterparts. This disparity can lead to financial vulnerability, especially when relationships end or when one partner passes away without a will. The proposed reforms seek to address these issues by introducing clearer and fairer provisions for cohabitants.

One of the key areas under review is the financial remedies available to cohabiting couples upon separation. The government is considering establishing a statutory framework that would outline the financial entitlements and obligations of cohabitants, providing greater clarity and security for individuals in these relationships.

Additionally, the consultation addresses the rules of intestacy—situations where a person dies without leaving a will. Under current law, cohabiting partners have no automatic right to inherit from their deceased partner's estate, which can result in significant hardship. The proposed changes aim to grant cohabitants a more equitable share of the estate, reflecting the nature and duration of their relationship.

These reforms are part of a broader effort to modernize family law in Scotland, ensuring it aligns with contemporary societal norms and provides adequate protection for all family structures. The government emphasizes the importance of public input in this process and encourages individuals, especially those directly affected, to participate in the consultation.
